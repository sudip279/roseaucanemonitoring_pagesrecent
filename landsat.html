Research Overview

Study Motivation & Scope

This research focused on Phragmites australis (Roseau cane) dieback assessment in the lower MRD. A comprehensive multi-source remote sensing method was developed across three scale levels — large (Landsat 30 m), mid (WorldView-2), and small (drone). Large-scale historical analyses using Landsat (2001–2021) identified a strong increasing vegetation trend since 2005.

Around 51 km² of dieback was detected, of which 65.8% occurred within Roseau cane areas — even though Roseau cane made up only 49.7% of total vegetation in 2010. This confirms significantly higher vulnerability compared to other marsh vegetation types in the lower MRD.

02

Vegetation Dieback Assessment — Landsat NDVI Change (2010–2019)

NDVI difference mapping between 2010 and 2019 classified vegetation dieback levels across the lower MRD. Most dieback and vegetation loss areas were located in the east and south margins of the delta. Stable vegetation (65.68%) represents the conservation baseline. The 20.98% gain areas in the north reflect sediment deposition and marsh protection processes.

WorldView-2 Mid-Scale Classification — Main Pass & South Pass

Roseau Cane Variant Separation (Delta vs. European Phragmites)

WorldView-2 SVM classification successfully distinguished Delta and European Phragmites variants across two passes in the lower MRD, with ~79% agreement with Landsat-derived results. Nine land cover classes were mapped. Delta Phragmites dominates at 80.8% of vegetation area; European Phragmites accounts for 2.7%. No significant dieback difference was found between the two variants (2019–2021). Each panel below shows results for four classifiers: (a) KNN, (b) MLC, (c) SVM — best performer, (d) RF, with (e) location inset.

04

Texture Feature Analysis (SVM + Texture Windows)

Texture features were added to SVM to test whether spatial context improves accuracy. The 7×7 window yielded the highest overall accuracy (91.95%, κ = 0.91) but introduced salt-and-pepper noise due to fragmented Roseau cane stands in high-resolution WV imagery. The marginal gain did not justify the noise — base SVM was selected for all production maps.
