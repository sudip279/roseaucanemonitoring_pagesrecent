Research Overview

Motivation & Approach

Phragmites australis (Roseau cane) is a dominant marsh species contributing substantially to sediment retention, shoreline stabilization, and overall wetland resilience in the lower MRD. Its distribution is highly dynamic, driven by fluctuations in hydrology, storm impacts, salinity intrusion, and episodic environmental stressors. Accurate and consistent annual mapping is essential for monitoring ecosystem health and supporting restoration planning.

Traditional machine learning classifiers are limited in capturing the spatial complexity and spectral variability of marsh vegetation — they are sensitive to seasonal changes, atmospheric conditions, and mixed pixel effects, producing inconsistent classifications across time. To address these limitations, this study applied a U-Net deep learning model (ResNet-34 encoder) to annual fall-season Sentinel-2 composites (2019–2024). U-Net's encoder-decoder architecture integrates spatial context at multiple scales, enabling robust pixel-level classification even under complex environmental conditions.

U-Net Model Configuration

A U-Net model with a ResNet-34 encoder was applied for pixel-level classification. Hyperparameters — chip size, batch size, epochs, learning rate, and loss function — were iteratively tuned for stable convergence and robust generalization.

Training data consisted of validated polygons for three classes prepared annually (2019–2024) using high-resolution NAIP and WorldView-2 imagery. Model performance was monitored through training loss, validation loss, and accuracy curves — showing consistent loss reduction and close alignment between training and validation trends across all years.

03

Classification Accuracy — Annual Performance (2019–2024)

The U-Net model achieved high and consistent performance across all six years. Overall accuracy ranged from 90% (2019) to 98% (2023), with Kappa from 0.85 to 0.98. Phragmites class accuracy ranged from 80% (2021 — disturbance year) to 96% (2023). Accuracy declines in 2021 and 2024 were linked to disturbance-induced habitat stress, not model limitations. Spatially, Phragmites was consistently mapped along distributary channels and marsh edges; minor errors occurred in fragmented interior areas.

Phragmites Area Dynamics — Disturbance & Recovery (2019–2024)

Annual classified Phragmites coverage shows clear sensitivity to environmental disturbances and strong post-disturbance recovery. Coverage declined significantly during disturbance years — driven by hurricanes, a historic freeze, and extreme drought — and rebounded rapidly in stable periods, confirming the species' intrinsic resilience in the lower MRD.

Key Findings

Model Performance

U-Net (ResNet-34 backbone) produced consistently high classification accuracy (90–98%) across all six years. Accuracy declines were linked to disturbance-induced habitat changes — not model limitations. Phragmites mapping is spatially reliable, especially along distributary channels and marsh edges where the species forms dense monoculture stands.

Ecological Insights

Phragmites area declined significantly during disturbance events (hurricanes, historic freeze, extreme drought) and recovered rapidly in stable periods. The species demonstrates high sensitivity to environmental stress and strong resilience — a pattern consistent across both hurricane-type and drought-type disturbances, suggesting an intrinsic recovery mechanism independent of disturbance type.
