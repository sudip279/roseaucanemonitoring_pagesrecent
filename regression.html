Assessing Roseau Cane Health via Scale Insect Density & Multi-Sensor NDVI Regression

Regression analysis correlating field-measured scale insect (Nipponaclerda biwakoensis) density with NDVI derived from four sensors — Landsat, Sentinel-2, PlanetScope, and drone orthophotos — across 65 sampling plots spanning the lower Mississippi River Delta.

01

Background & Study Gap

Why Roseau Cane Is Declining

Die-off of Roseau cane stands has been observed in the lower Mississippi River Delta since 2016. High density of a scale insect, Nipponaclerda biwakoensis, was discovered on stems — the insects feed on stem sap, ultimately killing the plant and leaving dead standing stems. Meanwhile, Louisiana loses approximately a football field of land every hour, and Roseau cane's extensive rhizome network has historically been critical for trapping sediment and stabilizing wetland soils.

Why This Study Was Needed

Previous research on Roseau cane scale insects was limited to single garden sites or small field plots. There was no established mathematical correlation between field-measured scale density and remotely sensed plant health indices across larger areas. This study aimed to fill that gap by building regression relationships between scale survey data and NDVI derived from multiple sensors at different spatial resolutions — spanning 2000 km² of the lower MRD.

02

Hypotheses

✓ Hypothesis 1 — Validated
With increasing scale insect density, Roseau cane health degrades — leading to a decrease in greenness and therefore a decrease in Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI).
Confirmed: A negative correlation between scale density and NDVI was found across all four sensor types and both seasons.
✗ Hypothesis 2 — Not Validated
Higher resolution remote sensing imagery will better classify individual plant health, producing a stronger correlation between plant health and scale infestation.
Contrary to expectation: coarser-resolution sensors (Landsat 30 m) showed stronger correlations than the finest resolution (drone ~8 cm). Mixed pixel effects at very high resolution require subpixel-level analysis before comparison is valid.

Field Scale Count Method

Roseau cane stems were randomly selected from inside a ring of 1.77 m perimeter (area = 0.25 m²) at each of 65 sampling plots spanning the lower MRD (~2000 km² coverage) and at 37 plots within the 0.2 km² Pass-A-Loutre drone site. Leaf sheaths were peeled off each stem to count medium, large, parasitized, and dead scales underneath. The total count per plot was averaged to yield average scale count per stem — the field metric used in all regressions.

Equipment: DJI Matrice 600 Pro with MicaSense RedEdge-MX dual camera (10 spectral bands). Flights at 120 m altitude, 75% overlap, 8 m/s. Orthomosaics at ~8 cm. Reflectance calibration via calibration panel before and after each flight. Agisoft Metashape for photogrammetric processing; ArcGIS Pro for NDVI derivation.

05

Results — Scale Density vs. NDVI Regression

Key Findings Across Sensors & Seasons

For all sensor types and both seasons (Summer and Fall 2023), increased scale insect density was associated with reduced NDVI — validating Hypothesis 1. Outlier removal (points obviously on water; subsequently, points on water and mixed pixels) improved regression fits across all sensors.

Landsat (30 m) showed the strongest correlation among the four sensors, contrary to the expectation that finer resolution would outperform. This is explained by the mixed pixel problem at very high resolution: the ~8 cm drone orthophoto captures individual stem-level variation, but plots cover 0.25 m² — meaning drone pixels may contain mixed vegetation types rather than pure Roseau cane, weakening the correlation signal.

Seasonal pattern: Fall 2023 produced stronger correlations than Summer 2023 across most sensors, consistent with end-of-season stress accumulation from scale feeding.

Per-Phenotype Analysis — Drone NDVI (Summer & Fall 2023)

Separate regression analyses were conducted for each Roseau cane phenotype (Delta and European Phragmites) using drone-derived NDVI at the Pass-A-Loutre site. Results showed phenotype-level variation in the scale density–NDVI relationship, though both phenotypes followed the general negative trend. The data represent a single year (2023) which was an extreme drought year — potentially introducing bias. Expanded multi-year sampling would improve generalizability.

06

Conclusions

✓ Confirmed Finding
Scale insect density and NDVI are negatively correlated — higher scale density corresponds to lower Roseau cane greenness and health across all sensor types and both seasons.
⚠ Requires Further Investigation
Higher spatial resolution does not automatically yield stronger scale–NDVI correlations. Mixed pixel effects at very fine resolution require subpixel-level analysis before pure Roseau cane NDVI can be reliably extracted for correlation.
07

Future Work

Future work will address the mixed pixel problem and expand the scope of the regression analysis:

1. Subpixel-level classification to find the percentage of area covered by pure Phragmites within each sensor pixel before computing NDVI for regression.

2. Analysis of pure Roseau cane distributions — sites such as Rockefeller Wildlife Refuge have minimal understory vegetation, making them ideal for testing the resolution hypothesis without mixed pixel interference.

3. Investigation of additional environmental factors — elevation, flooding frequency, and their effects on scale insect density in a remote sensing framework.
